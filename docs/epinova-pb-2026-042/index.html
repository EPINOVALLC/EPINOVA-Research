Beyond Hormuz: Iran’s Ten-Corridor Logistics Adaptation under Blockade Pressure

  • Wu, Shaoyuan
    Global AI Governance and Policy Research Center, EPINOVA LLC
    https://orcid.org/0009-0008-0660-8232

Description

This policy brief examines Iran’s reported ten-corridor logistics adaptation under blockade pressure. It analyzes how alternative land-sea corridors, Caspian shipping, Central Asian rail connectivity, air replenishment routes, and residual Hormuz/Persian Gulf movement combine into a multi-domain, threshold-delaying supply system.

Abstract

This policy brief assesses Iran’s reported plan to develop ten distinct alternative trade corridors under blockade pressure. It argues that the plan should not be understood as a replacement for the Persian Gulf and Strait of Hormuz logistics system, but as a distributed resilience strategy designed to preserve minimum viable flows. Because Iran has not released a full corridor-by-corridor list, the brief reconstructs a plausible ten-corridor framework based on public reporting, geography, trade patterns, and observed logistics behavior. It further shows that Iran’s adaptation extends beyond land and sea routes: air connectivity adds a high-value replenishment layer, while residual movement through a degraded but not fully sealed Hormuz/Persian Gulf window preserves limited maritime flow. The brief concludes that Iran’s ten-corridor adaptation does not end the blockade problem; it turns it into a long-duration contest of logistics friction.
